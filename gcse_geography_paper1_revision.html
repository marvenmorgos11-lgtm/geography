SECTION A — THE CHALLENGE OF NATURAL HAZARDS

A1 · Tectonic Hazards

Plate Boundaries

Boundary TypeMovementHazardsExample
DestructiveOceanic sinks under continental (subduction)Powerful earthquakes + explosive volcanoesAndes, Japan
ConstructivePlates move apart (diverge)Earthquakes + gentle volcanoesMid-Atlantic Ridge, Iceland
ConservativePlates slide past each otherEarthquakes ONLY (no volcanoes)San Andreas Fault, USA
CollisionTwo continental plates collideFold mountains, earthquakes (no volcanoes)Himalayas
Conservative = NO volcanoes. Plates slide sideways — no magma rises. This is a favourite trick question.

Why People Live Near Tectonic Hazards

  • Fertile volcanic soil (e.g. farming on Java, Indonesia)
  • Geothermal energy (Iceland)
  • Tourism (Hawaii)
  • Lack of choice / poverty — can't afford to move
  • Short return period — hasn't happened in their lifetime, so risk feels low

Effects of Earthquakes: Primary vs Secondary

Primary (immediate, caused directly by shaking)
  • Buildings collapse
  • Roads/bridges crack
  • People killed/injured
  • Gas pipes rupture
Secondary (follow-on effects)
  • Fires from broken gas pipes
  • Tsunamis
  • Landslides
  • Disease from contaminated water

Responses: Immediate vs Long-Term

Immediate (within days/weeks)
  • Search and rescue
  • Emergency food, water, shelter
  • Field hospitals
  • International aid sent
Long-term (months/years)
  • Rebuilding homes and infrastructure
  • Improved building regulations (earthquake-proof buildings)
  • Monitoring systems installed
  • Economic recovery plans
Nepal Earthquake 2015 (LIC/NEE) vs Chile Earthquake 2010 (HIC)
Nepal: 9,000 killed, 3.5 million homeless, poor infrastructure, slow aid delivery, mountainous terrain blocked rescue. Long-term: $10bn reconstruction needed.
Chile: 500 killed (better building codes), quick government response, early warning systems existed, rapid rebuilding. Shows how wealth = fewer deaths.
Montserrat Volcano 1995–97 (LIC)
Soufrière Hills erupted. Southern half of island abandoned. Capital Plymouth buried in ash. 19 deaths. Immediate: evacuation zones set up. Long-term: northern half redeveloped, tourism increased (volcano tourism). Economy slowly recovered with UK aid.

A2 · Weather Hazards — Tropical Storms

How Tropical Storms Form

  1. Form over warm ocean water (above 27°C)
  2. Warm, moist air rises rapidly → low pressure created
  3. Water vapour condenses → releases energy → storms intensify
  4. Earth's rotation (Coriolis effect) causes spinning
  5. Storms weaken when they hit land — no more warm water = no fuel
They form between 5°–20° north/south of equator. NOT at the equator (no Coriolis effect there).

Structure of a Tropical Storm

  • Eye — calm centre, low pressure, clear skies, light winds
  • Eyewall — most violent winds and rainfall, surrounds the eye
  • Spiral rainbands — heavy rain extending outwards

Effects & Responses

Typhoon Haiyan, Philippines 2013 (LIC/NEE)
Effects: 6,300+ killed, 4 million homeless, 90% of Tacloban destroyed, storm surge 7m high, crops destroyed, disease outbreaks.
Immediate: International aid (UN, UK, USA), military helicopters, emergency food drops.
Long-term: Rebuilding took years, new sea defences, better warning systems, mangrove replanting.

Reducing the Effects of Tropical Storms

MonitoringSatellites track storm paths; wind speed measured; storm surges predicted
PredictionComputer models forecast path 3–5 days ahead; Hurricane Watch (USA) gives 48hr warnings
ProtectionSea walls, storm shelters, building regulations (raised houses), mangroves reduce wave energy

A3 · Climate Change

Evidence for Climate Change

  • Global temperatures risen ~1.2°C since pre-industrial times
  • Ice cores show CO₂ levels higher than at any point in 800,000 years
  • Glaciers retreating worldwide
  • Sea levels rising (~3.3mm/year)
  • Arctic sea ice shrinking

Causes: Natural vs Human

Natural causes
  • Milankovitch cycles — changes in Earth's orbit around the Sun
  • Solar output — sunspot cycles (11-year cycle)
  • Volcanic eruptions — ash reflects sunlight (short-term cooling); CO₂ (long-term warming)
Human causes (Enhanced Greenhouse Effect)
  • Burning fossil fuels → CO₂
  • Deforestation → less CO₂ absorbed
  • Agriculture → methane (cattle, rice paddies)
  • Landfill → methane
  • Industry → nitrous oxide

Effects of Climate Change

Physical effects
  • Sea level rise → coastal flooding
  • More extreme weather events (storms, droughts)
  • Ice caps melting
  • Coral bleaching
  • Ecosystem disruption (timing of seasons shifts)
Human effects
  • Flooding displaces millions (e.g. Bangladesh)
  • Water/food shortages in dry regions
  • Spread of diseases (malaria moving to higher altitudes)
  • Economic damage from extreme weather
  • New opportunities — farming in Arctic areas, new shipping routes

Managing Climate Change

Mitigation (reducing causes)
  • Renewable energy (wind, solar, HEP)
  • Carbon capture and storage
  • Reforestation
  • International agreements (Paris Agreement 2015)
  • Energy efficiency in buildings/transport
Adaptation (adjusting to effects)
  • Flood defences (sea walls, flood barriers)
  • Drought-resistant crops
  • Managed retreat of coastlines
  • Building designs to cope with heat/floods
  • Water conservation schemes

SECTION B — THE LIVING WORLD

B1 · Ecosystems

Key Terms

  • Ecosystem — a community of living organisms interacting with their non-living environment
  • Producer — plants that photosynthesise (make their own food)
  • Consumer — animals that eat producers or other animals
  • Decomposer — break down dead matter (fungi, bacteria)
  • Food chain/web — shows energy transfer between organisms
  • Nutrient cycle — how nutrients move through ecosystem (living things → decomposers → soil → plants)
In a healthy ecosystem, if one part is removed, it affects ALL other parts. E.g. remove top predator → prey population explodes → plants are overgrazed → ecosystem collapses.

Global Ecosystems (Biomes)

Climate determines which biome exists where. Temperature and rainfall are the key controls.

Tropical RainforestHot & wet all yearAround the equator
Hot DesertHot days, cold nights, very dry~30°N/S of equator
Savanna GrasslandHot, wet + dry seasonsBetween rainforest & desert
Temperate DeciduousWarm summers, mild wintersWestern Europe, NE USA

B2 · Tropical Rainforests

Characteristics

  • Found near the equator (0°–10° N/S) — e.g. Amazon, Congo, Borneo
  • Hot (~28°C) and wet (2000mm+ rain/year) all year round
  • Layered structure: emergent layer → canopy → understorey → forest floor
  • Highest biodiversity on Earth — over 50% of all species

Plant Adaptations

  • Drip-tip leaves — pointed tips so water drips off (prevents rot, fungal growth)
  • Buttress roots — large roots at base support massive trees in shallow soil
  • Smooth/waxy bark — sheds water quickly
  • Epiphytes — plants that grow on other plants to reach sunlight (not parasitic)

Animal Adaptations

  • Bright colours (warning/camouflage in dappled light)
  • Prehensile tails / long limbs for life in canopy
  • Nocturnal species to avoid competition

Nutrient Cycle in TRF

Nutrients cycle rapidly. Decomposition is fast (warm+wet). Most nutrients in biomass (living plants), NOT in the soil. So if trees are removed, the nutrient cycle breaks and soil becomes infertile quickly.

Deforestation — Causes

Commercial farming — soya beans, cattle ranching (Brazil)
Logging — hardwood timber (mahogany, teak) for export
Mining — iron ore, gold (Amazon)
Road building — opens up access to more forest; settlement follows

Impacts of Deforestation

  • Loss of biodiversity (species extinction)
  • Soil erosion — roots no longer hold soil; heavy rain washes it away
  • Increased CO₂ — trees no longer absorb it; burning releases it → climate change
  • Indigenous people lose their homes and culture
  • Changes to water cycle — less transpiration → less rainfall
Amazon Rainforest, Brazil
17% deforested since 1970. Causes: cattle ranching (70% of cleared land), soya farming, mining (Carajás iron ore), BR-163 highway. Government introduced fines for illegal logging; REDD+ scheme pays countries to protect forest; sustainable logging (FSC certified timber); ecotourism provides income without destroying forest.

Sustainable Management

  • Selective logging — only mature trees cut, rest left standing
  • Agroforestry — trees grown alongside crops, maintains soil
  • Ecotourism — income from tourists visiting forest, no destruction
  • Debt relief — rich countries cancel debts if poor countries protect forests
  • REDD+ — UN scheme paying developing nations to reduce deforestation
  • National Parks / reserves — protected areas with legal status

B3 · Hot Deserts (study if your school chose this option)

Characteristics

  • Receive less than 250mm rainfall per year
  • Extreme temperatures — very hot days (50°C+), cold nights
  • Found ~30° N/S of equator (high pressure zones — sinking dry air)
  • Example: Sahara (Africa), Sonoran (USA/Mexico), Arabian Desert

Plant Adaptations

  • Cacti — thick waxy skin (reduces water loss), spines (not leaves), stores water in stem, shallow wide roots catch rain quickly
  • Succulents — store water in fleshy leaves/stems
  • Deep roots (e.g. mesquite tree) — reach underground water
  • Seeds that lie dormant for years, germinate only when it rains

Animal Adaptations

  • Nocturnal — active at night to avoid heat
  • Camels — store fat in hump (not water), thick eyelashes, wide feet, concentrate urine
  • Fennec fox — large ears dissipate heat

Development Opportunities in Deserts

  • Mineral extraction — oil (Saudi Arabia), copper, gold
  • Energy — solar power (intense sunlight), wind power
  • Tourism — unique landscapes attract tourists (Sahara safaris)
  • Farming — irrigation schemes (e.g. Colorado River, Nile)

Challenges of Desert Development

  • Extreme heat makes working difficult
  • Water scarcity — expensive to import/desalinate
  • Inaccessibility — sparse infrastructure, high transport costs
  • Fragile ecosystem — easily damaged, slow to recover
Thar Desert, India — Development & Challenges
Indira Gandhi Canal brings water for irrigation → farming, settlement. Mineral reserves (feldspar, gypsum). Wind/solar farms expanding. Challenges: salinisation of soils from irrigation, overgrazing leading to desertification, over-extraction of groundwater.

Desertification

The process by which land at the edge of a desert becomes desert-like and unproductive.

  • Causes: overgrazing, deforestation, climate change (less rain), over-cultivation, population pressure
  • Management: tree planting (Great Green Wall project across Africa), water conservation, soil conservation, education about land use, appropriate technology

SECTION C — PHYSICAL LANDSCAPES IN THE UK

C1 · Coastal Landscapes in the UK

Coastal Processes

Erosion processes (HHAC)
  • Hydraulic action — force of water/air compresses into cracks, breaking rock apart
  • Abrasion — sand and pebbles scrape/grind rock face
  • Attrition — rocks/pebbles knock against each other, getting smaller and rounder
  • Corrosion — chemical weathering, acid in seawater dissolves limestone
Transportation (STSC)
  • Solution — dissolved minerals carried in water
  • Suspension — fine particles carried in water
  • Saltation — material bounced along seabed
  • Traction — large boulders rolled along seabed

Longshore drift — waves hit beach at an angle (swash), backwash goes straight back. Material transported along the coast in a zigzag pattern. Direction depends on prevailing wind.

Swash = up the beach at an angle. Backwash = straight back perpendicular. This is how material moves along the coast.

Erosional Landforms

LandformHow it forms
Headland & BayAlternating hard and soft rock. Soft rock erodes faster → bay. Hard rock resists → headland protrudes.
Cave → Arch → Stack → StumpWaves erode crack in headland → cave. Erosion from both sides → arch. Arch collapses → stack. Stack erodes → stump.
Wave-cut platformCliff eroded at base → notch → cliff collapses → platform left behind at sea level

Depositional Landforms

LandformHow it forms
BeachSand/pebbles deposited when waves lose energy
SpitLongshore drift extends a beach beyond a bend in the coastline. End curves due to secondary wind. May form across a bay.
BarSpit grows all the way across a bay, enclosing a lagoon
Sand dunesWind deposits sand above high tide mark; marram grass stabilises

Coastal Management Strategies

Hard Engineering
  • Sea wall — reflects waves, expensive (£2,000/m), lasts ~30 yrs
  • Groynes — wooden fences trap sediment, prevents longshore drift removing beach
  • Rock armour (rip rap) — large boulders absorb wave energy, cheap but ugly
  • Offshore breakwater — rocks offshore, waves break before reaching shore
Soft Engineering
  • Beach nourishment — sand pumped from offshore to replace eroded beach. Natural-looking but needs repeating.
  • Managed retreat — allow flooding of low-value land, creates saltmarsh that absorbs wave energy. Cheap, sustainable.
  • Dune stabilisation — plant marram grass to hold dunes
Holderness Coast, Yorkshire — Rapid Erosion
Fastest eroding coast in Europe (~2m/year). Soft boulder clay easily eroded. Settlements at risk: Mappleton protected with rock groynes (1991, £2m) — saved village but accelerated erosion south of Mappleton. Spurn Head at risk. Debate: protect everywhere vs managed retreat.

C2 · River Landscapes in the UK

River Processes

  • Erosion — Hydraulic action, Abrasion, Attrition, Corrosion (same as coasts)
  • Transportation — Traction, Saltation, Suspension, Solution
  • Deposition — when river loses energy (e.g. velocity decreases), it drops sediment — heaviest first

Long Profile & Cross Profile

  • Upper course — steep gradient, fast flow in small channel, V-shaped valley, lots of vertical erosion
  • Middle course — gentler gradient, lateral (sideways) erosion, wider valley, meanders form
  • Lower course — very gentle gradient, wide flat floodplain, lots of deposition, mouth/estuary

Landforms — Upper Course

LandformFormation
WaterfallRiver flows over hard rock onto soft rock. Soft rock undercut by hydraulic action/abrasion → forms plunge pool. Overhang collapses. Waterfall retreats upstream leaving a gorge.
GorgeNarrow steep-sided valley left behind as waterfall retreats
Interlocking spursRiver winds around hard rock outcrops in V-shaped valley

Landforms — Middle & Lower Course

LandformFormation
MeandersWater flows faster on outer bank (erosion = river cliff) and slower on inner bank (deposition = slip-off slope/point bar)
Ox-bow lakeMeander neck eroded through during flood. River takes straight course. Old meander cut off. Evaporation forms ox-bow lake.
FloodplainFlat land either side of river, built up from sediment deposited in repeated floods. Very fertile.
LevéesNatural embankments of deposited material along river banks (heaviest material deposited first as river slows during flooding)
Estuary/deltaWhere river meets sea. Sediment deposited. Tidal. May form delta if river drops more sediment than sea removes.

Factors Affecting Flood Risk

Increases flood risk
  • Heavy/prolonged rainfall
  • Impermeable rock/soil
  • Urbanisation (concrete = no infiltration)
  • Deforestation (less interception)
  • Steep slopes (fast run-off)
  • Saturated ground
Decreases flood risk
  • Permeable rock (water soaks in)
  • Vegetation (interception + transpiration)
  • Gentle slopes (slow run-off)
  • Dry ground (can absorb water)

River Management

Hard Engineering
  • Dams & reservoirs — control water flow, provide water/HEP, but flood land, displace people
  • Flood relief channels — divert excess water away from towns
  • Embankments — raise river banks
  • Channel straightening — water moves faster away from settlements
Soft Engineering
  • Flood warnings — Environment Agency alerts
  • Flood plain zoning — no building on floodplain
  • Afforestation — plant trees in catchment to intercept rain
  • River restoration — re-meander rivers, create wetlands
Boscastle Flash Flood 2004, Cornwall, UK
200mm rain fell in 24 hours (normally 75mm in August). Steep impermeable valley, saturated ground. River Valency burst banks. 100 cars washed out to sea, buildings destroyed. No deaths (rapid helicopter rescue). Responses: new flood channel, culverts widened, car park moved to higher ground.

EXAM TIPS — READ BEFORE YOU SIT

Question command words
  • State/Name/Identify — one word/phrase answer
  • Describe — what it looks like (use data/figures)
  • Explain — say WHY / HOW (use "because", "which means", "therefore")
  • Assess/Evaluate/Discuss — give both sides + a conclusion
  • To what extent — make a judgement with evidence
Mark allocation guide
  • 1–2 marks — one/two facts. No explanation needed.
  • 4 marks — two developed points (point + explanation each)
  • 6 marks — structured answer using a figure + own knowledge. Two or three linked points per side.
  • 9 marks — full essay. Introduction, 2–3 paragraphs, conclusion. SPaG marks here — spell properly!
Case study rules
  • Always name specific places, dates, and figures
  • Know one LIC/NEE and one HIC example for tectonic hazards and tropical storms
  • For 9-mark questions: use case studies to back up every argument
Common mistakes to avoid
  • Confusing erosion types (hydraulic action ≠ abrasion)
  • Saying conservative margins have volcanoes — they DON'T
  • Describing when asked to explain (always say WHY)
  • Corrie = rotational slip, NOT just "ice melting"
  • Forgetting to use data from figures in 6-mark questions
